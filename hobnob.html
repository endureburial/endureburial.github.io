Hob Nobs

I once knew a Hobnob. He occupied a shack on the Heath and lived on a diet of pulses and wild rabbits. I met him on a crisp January morning. He was crouching down in the autumn leaves, aiming an air rifle at a careless hare. The little creature was soon to be his lunch yet stood among the dewdropped wildflowers and ladybirds with a look of tragic innocence. Although hardened, this Hobnob had not lost his kindness. The oatie goodness of his heart relaxed his limps and averted his focus. As he stood up from the leaves, the hare left its blissful daze and sprung across the meadow into safety.

Since that day, I often sat with him during my excursions to the Heath. I enjoyed the glimpse of rustic hermitage that this Hobnob offered. He gifted me an insight into a world of isolation and contented penury. As I ponder now, in the night-time quiet of my Kensington property, the essence of that man brings a tear to my eye.
